Programs & How They Work Together

TYI operates as a single integrated model: direct programmatic support in Dharamsala informs, and is informed by, substantive policy research submitted to international institutions. Neither track exists in isolation.

Integrated program model
  1. Partner schools identify student material needs and programmatic priorities.
  2. TYI coordinates supply procurement, distribution, and classroom delivery.
  3. On-the-ground experience informs research questions and policy briefs.
  4. Policy submissions to UN, EU, and technology institutions advance systemic protections for youth.
  5. International advocacy feeds back into local program design and resource allocation.

Educational Access

TYI’s foundational program distributes learning materials, notebooks, writing instruments, art supplies, and organizational tools, to three partner schools in Dharamsala. In 2025 alone, 36,409 items reached more than 1,500 students across Tibetan Children’s Village, Mewoen Tsuglag Petoen School, and Yongling Crèche & Kindergarten.

Distribution is needs-based: partner schools communicate classroom requirements, TYI procures materials through donor support, and supplies are delivered directly to classrooms. This closes a persistent gap between enrollment and material readiness, students cannot fully participate when basic resources are unavailable.

Cultural Preservation

Tibetan language instruction and heritage programming are central to exile education. TYI supports schools that integrate modern curricula with Tibetan cultural education, ensuring students maintain linguistic and identity continuity while pursuing academic advancement.

Material support for arts instruction, drawing books, colored pencils, and classroom resources, directly sustains cultural programming embedded in partner school curricula. This programmatic layer connects to TYI’s minority rights research, which documents institutional barriers facing linguistic minorities in diaspora contexts.

Policy Advocacy & Research

TYI submits INFO REPORTS to international bodies including the UN Office of the High Commissioner for Human Rights, the European Commission, OpenAI, Anthropic, and the Digital Regulation Cooperation Forum. Research areas include:

  • Human rights and minority protections: linguistic, cultural, and institutional barriers in diaspora education
  • Youth mental health: rights-based frameworks for equitable access to care
  • Displaced youth education: material security, youth agency, and digital governance
  • Transitional justice and technology: digital tools for documentation and accountability
  • Algorithmic safety: cognitive vulnerabilities of youth in generative AI systems
  • AI governance: supply chain dependencies in frontier model development

Field experience in Dharamsala grounds these submissions in the lived conditions of Tibetan youth in exile. Policy recommendations are not abstract, they reflect observable gaps in material access, cultural support, and institutional recognition.

School Partnerships

TYI maintains direct relationships with partner institutions rather than operating through intermediaries. This allows responsive allocation: when a school reports a classroom need, TYI can act without bureaucratic delay. Partnerships expanded in 2024 to include three schools serving distinct age groups, from early childhood through secondary education, creating coverage across the developmental spectrum.

Community Support & Sustainability

All raised funds are deployed to direct programmatic activities, supply procurement, distribution logistics, and research production. TYI is sustained by individual donors whose contributions enable both classroom-level impact and international advocacy.
