INFO REPORT: New Technologies and Transitional Justice

Submitted to
“Input on New Technologies and Transitional Justice” of the Office of the United Nations High Commissioner for Human Rights
Authors
Nyida Gyal

[1] Executive Summary

  • New digital and AI-enabled tools are already shaping documentation, truth-seeking and accountability in transitional justice processes.
  • AI-assisted open-source investigations and satellite imagery can strengthen fact-finding when embedded in expert-led workflows, not crowd-sourced vigilantism (Mukhopadhyay et al., 2024; Edler et al., 2025).
  • Evidence also shows serious risks: misidentification, biased or misleading inferences, deepfakes and synthetic media, and systematic over-trust in AI outputs, even when systems are labelled as “advisory” (Agudo et al., 2024).
  • “Human-in-the-loop” safeguards, on their own, do not reliably prevent harmful errors, because human decision-makers tend to anchor on initial AI suggestions and struggle to override them (Agudo et al., 2024).
  • Conceptually and legally, AI systems cannot bear epistemic or moral responsibility; responsibility must remain with identifiable human and institutional actors (Sparrow & Flenady, 2025).
  • OHCHR and transitional justice mechanisms should adopt clear safeguards: provenance and chain-of-custody standards for digital evidence, bans on automated attribution of individual criminal responsibility, mandatory uncertainty and error disclosure, independent audits and red-teaming, robust witness protection and data minimisation, and human-rights due diligence duties for technology providers (Loffi et al., 2023; Mukhopadhyay et al., 2024).

[2] Opportunities

New technologies already play a role across the transitional justice lifecycle, from documentation during conflict to later truth commissions and criminal accountability. When carefully governed, they can expand the evidentiary base, corroborate survivor testimonies, and counter denial.

AI-assisted open-source investigations (OSINT)

AI tools can help human investigators triage and structure vast quantities of open-source material—videos, images, social media posts and sensor data—produced during crises and conflicts. Mukhopadhyay et al. (2024) show that when OSINT is organised through expert-supervised workflows, with transparent sourcing and verification steps, AI-assisted search and classification can make investigations more systematic and less dependent on ad hoc networks. This is particularly valuable in contexts where traditional access is blocked, and where digital traces may be the only route to establishing patterns of violence.

Satellite imagery and remote sensing

Satellite imagery and other remote sensing modalities have been used to identify mass displacement, destruction of civilian infrastructure and suspected mass graves in conflict and post-conflict settings. Edler et al. (2025) document how such imagery, combined with ground information, can corroborate witness accounts and reveal patterns that would otherwise be invisible, such as the timing and extent of village burnings or the construction of detention facilities. These methods can support early warning, preserve evidence before it is physically destroyed, and provide a relatively impartial record where access is politically constrained.

Digital archiving and integrity mechanisms

Digital evidence—including testimonies, audiovisual files and sensor data—requires secure and tamper-evident storage. Loffi et al. (2023) review blockchain-based and related integrity systems being explored in evidence management and codes of conduct, highlighting their potential to provide transparent, time-stamped chains of custody. In transitional justice, such infrastructures can help ensure that digital records used in later proceedings can be authenticated, and that subsequent manipulation is detectable.

Used under strict safeguards, these technologies can support victims’ rights to truth and accountability, particularly where conventional investigative capacities are limited. However, the same tools can also generate false certainty or be repurposed for surveillance and repression, underscoring the importance of clear limitations.

[3] Risks and Limitations

The existing evidence base underscores that the main dangers arise not only from technical errors, but from how humans and institutions interact with AI systems.

Over-reliance and “human-in-the-loop” failures

Experimental work by Agudo et al. (2024) demonstrates that human decision-makers frequently anchor on erroneous AI recommendations, even when they know that the system is fallible and advisory. Exposure to an incorrect AI suggestion significantly reduces overall accuracy compared with unaided human judgment, because people struggle to appropriately down-weight the AI’s influence. This suggests that simply adding a nominal “human in the loop” does not, by itself, guarantee safety or accountability in truth-seeking or prosecutorial decisions.

Bias, misidentification and coverage gaps

Edler et al. (2025) highlight limitations in satellite-based human rights monitoring: spatial and temporal coverage is uneven, cloud cover and image resolution constrain detection, and algorithmic analysis can misclassify features. These technical constraints interact with political and commercial priorities, potentially creating blind spots in marginalised regions. Similarly, Mukhopadhyay et al. (2024) warn that OSINT investigations can reflect the biases of digital platforms and connectivity: communities with less online presence or limited access to smartphones are less visible in the evidentiary record.

Responsibility gaps and over-trust

Sparrow and Flenady (2025) argue that AI systems, including LLMs and automated analytic tools, cannot be bearers of epistemic or moral responsibility. Treating “the algorithm” as responsible risks diffusing accountability away from specific officials, experts, and institutions. At the same time, psychological evidence indicates that apparently precise outputs—heatmaps, confidence scores, polished textual summaries—can invite over-trust, especially among non-technical users. Transitional justice processes, which already contend with power asymmetries and trauma, are particularly vulnerable to such dynamics.

Security, privacy, and risks to witnesses

Digital evidence infrastructures also create new vectors of harm. Large-scale collection and centralisation of testimonies, biometrics, and location data can expose survivors and witnesses to retaliation if security measures fail or political contexts change. Loffi et al. (2023) emphasise that technical integrity solutions must be embedded within broader legal and ethical frameworks that prioritise data minimisation, access controls, and clear deletion or anonymisation policies.

[4] Safeguards and Recommendations

In light of these opportunities and risks, OHCHR and transitional justice mechanisms should adopt the following safeguards:

  1. Provenance and chain-of-custody standards for digital evidence. All digital evidence used in transitional justice processes should include documented provenance, with standardised metadata on source, collection method, transformations, and storage. Integrity mechanisms (e.g., cryptographic hashing, distributed ledgers) may be used as supporting tools, but always within legal chain-of-custody procedures (Loffi et al., 2023).
  2. No automated attribution of individual criminal responsibility. AI systems should not be used to automatically attribute individual criminal responsibility, identify suspects, or generate lists of alleged perpetrators. Automated outputs may inform investigative leads but must never be treated as evidence of guilt. Attribution decisions should remain with identifiable human investigators, judges, and prosecutors (Sparrow & Flenady, 2025).
  3. Error and uncertainty disclosure. Any use of AI-assisted analysis (e.g., satellite-based damage assessment, OSINT triage, automated transcription) should include clear disclosure of known error rates, coverage gaps, and uncertainty. Reports and public communications should avoid presenting AI-derived findings as definitive, and should explicitly describe limitations noted in the literature (Edler et al., 2025; Mukhopadhyay et al., 2024).
  4. Robust human oversight and independent review. Where AI outputs inform key decisions (e.g., which incidents to investigate, which locations to inspect), oversight should be exercised by trained experts who can critically interrogate the tools and their assumptions. Independent audits, red-teaming exercises, and periodic external reviews should be mandated to identify systematic biases and failure modes (Agudo et al., 2024).
  5. Witness protection and data minimisation. Digital evidence practices should follow a “minimum necessary data” principle. Sensitive personal data should be collected only when strictly required, stored with strong security safeguards, and subject to clear retention limits. Particular care is needed with biometric identifiers, geolocation, and any information that could enable targeting of survivors, witnesses, or human rights defenders.
  6. Corporate human-rights due diligence. Technology providers whose products are used in transitional justice or conflict-affected settings should be subject to human-rights due diligence obligations, consistent with the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights. This includes assessing and mitigating risks of misuse, providing transparency about system capabilities and limitations, and cooperating with independent audits and oversight (Mukhopadhyay et al., 2024).
