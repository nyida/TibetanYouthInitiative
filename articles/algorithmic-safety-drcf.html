Independent Policy Brief: Algorithmic Safety and Cognitive Vulnerabilities in Generative AI

Submitted to
The Digital Regulation Cooperation Forum (DRCF) Call for Input: Consumer Interest, Algorithmic Safety, and Generative AI Interfaces
Authors
Nyida Gyal, Thomas Millward

I. Executive Summary

As conversational and generative artificial intelligence frameworks rapidly integrate into consumer software, regulatory oversight bodies must assess the hidden systemic risks these models pose to vulnerable demographics. While current market interventions focus heavily on commercial data privacy and financial fraud, the deployment of interactive AI agents introduces a deeper, unmitigated vulnerability: cognitive and linguistic friction. The Tibetan Youth Initiative (TYI) provides this independent stakeholder response to the DRCF’s open call for input. Drawing on frameworks from cognitive science and international educational deployment, this brief outlines how unstructured multi-agent AI interfaces present cognitive development risks for multilingual and minority-language youth, and proposes three actionable safety principles for consumer AI protection.

II. The Core Challenge: Cognitive Vulnerabilities in Generative AI Tutoring

When children and young students interact with conversational AI interfaces marketed as educational or consumer assistants, they are interacting with architectures trained predominantly on high-resource, English-centric datasets. From a cognitive science perspective, this architectural monoculture introduces distinct developmental harms:

  • Cognitive Over-Reliance and Fact-Blending: Developing minds are highly susceptible to automation bias. When frontier models “hallucinate” or present distorted logic in low-resource language translations, children lack the cognitive metadata to verify the error. This leads to the early internalisation of corrupted facts and logical fallacies.
  • Linguistic Decay in Multilingual Syntaxes: Multi-agent systems frequently struggle with shifting language registers, cross-script parsing, and localized idioms. For bilingual youth, constant exposure to structurally broken native-language AI outputs induces linguistic friction, disrupting natural language retention and cognitive confidence.
  • Behavioral Reinforcement Loops: Consumer AI chat interfaces are structurally optimized for engagement rather than pedagogical efficacy. Chatbots that utilize gamified anthropomorphic framing can create addictive feedback loops, distorting a child’s social interaction schemas and cognitive attention spans.

III. Systemic Interface Design as a Consumer Safeguard

Protecting the consumer interests of young users requires moving beyond static privacy policies toward active, cognitive-first design constraints. An AI model that merely outputs raw text without guardrails is unsafe for child-facing educational distribution. Because grassroots student groups and independent software developers are rapidly building thin wrappers around frontier APIs, the responsibility to enforce safety must be structurally embedded into the interface layer. Regulators must establish clear, baseline expectations separating raw enterprise developer APIs from consumer-facing conversational software intended for instructional use.

IV. Actionable Recommendations for the DRCF Framework

We recommend that the joint regulators of the DRCF integrate the following three design and policy standards into their upcoming consumer protection guidelines:

  1. Mandate Clear Age-Appropriate “Hallucination Indicators”

    Consumer AI applications marketed for educational or child use should be required to feature prominent, real-time visual UI indicators signaling the probabilistic nature of the output. When a model switches from retrieving verified database facts to generating creative text, the interface must make that architectural shift completely transparent to the user.

  2. Establish Voluntary “Cognitive Safety Ratings” for Educational Apps

    The DRCF should pioneer a voluntary safety rating matrix evaluating how well consumer AI tutoring tools mitigate cognitive biases, protect data privacy for minors, and maintain high-fidelity translation syntax in low-resource scripts. This empowers parents and educational non-profits to easily audit software before deploying it.

  3. Enforce “Safe-by-Design” Safeguards on Open API Configurations

    Commercial AI labs hosting frontier APIs should be encouraged to provide native, easily toggleable “Child Safety Filters” at the API layer. This would allow independent, zero-margin developers and youth-led non-profits to build custom apps that are automatically hard-coded against generating toxic, addictive, or logically corrupted responses.
