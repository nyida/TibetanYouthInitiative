INFO REPORT: The Impact of Mental Health Challenges on the Enjoyment of Human Rights by Young People

Submitted to
“Input on the Impact of Mental Health Challenges on the Enjoyment of Human Rights by Young People” of the Office of the United Nations High Commissioner for Human Rights
Authors
Khadotso Gyal, Nyida Gyal*

[1] Framing: Youth Mental Health as a Human Rights Issue

Mental health conditions affect approximately one in five children and adolescents globally, and an estimated 75% of lifetime mental disorders begin before the age of 24, making youth mental health central to the enjoyment of the rights to health, development, education, participation, and non-discrimination (Kessler et al., 2007; WHO, 2021). Evidence indicates that rising levels of mental distress among adolescents and emerging adults represent a sustained global challenge, rather than improved detection alone (Erskine et al., 2023).

Structural underinvestment, discrimination in access to care, and harmful digital, educational, and economic environments create systemic barriers to the enjoyment of human rights by young people, particularly those in marginalized situations. These barriers reflect policy and governance failures, rather than individual weakness or vulnerability (WHO & UNICEF, 2021; Patel et al., 2018).

[2] Main Mental Health Challenges and Impairment of Specific Rights

Burden and trends

Mental and substance use disorders account for a substantial share of disability among young people, comprising approximately 15–25% of disability-adjusted life years (DALYs) in those under 20 in several high-income countries (GBD 2019 Mental Disorders Collaborators, 2022). Suicide rates among adolescents aged 10–19 have increased in multiple jurisdictions, while depression, anxiety, self-harm, and substance use remain prevalent across regions (WHO, 2023).

Right to health (including mental health)

Youth mental health needs remain largely unmet worldwide. Access to affordable, acceptable, and youth-friendly services is limited in both high-income countries and low- and middle-income countries (LMICs) (WHO, 2021). In parts of the Western Pacific and other LMIC regions, most countries allocate ≤1% of health expenditure to mental health, with only a small fraction dedicated to children and adolescents (WHO, 2022).

Right to education

Mental disorders during adolescence significantly interfere with learning, school attendance, and completion, undermining the right to education and long-term economic participation (Patel et al., 2018). While some education systems have begun integrating wellbeing frameworks, coverage and quality remain uneven and insufficiently evaluated (OECD, 2021).

Right to non-discrimination and equality

LGBTQI+ youth experience disproportionately high rates of depression, self-harm, and suicidality linked to stigma, victimization, and exclusion, reflecting structural discrimination (The Trevor Project, 2023; Meyer, 2003). Racialized youth, including Black youth in Canada and other contexts, face persistent barriers related to geography, cost, and discrimination within services, contradicting commitments to universal and equal access (Dryden & Nnorom, 2021).

Right to participation

Meaningful youth participation in civic and governance processes can support wellbeing and empowerment when structures are safe and supportive (UN OHCHR, 2020). However, tokenistic or poorly designed participation mechanisms may generate stress and mixed mental health outcomes, indicating that participation rights require protective safeguards (Checkoway & Richards-Schuster, 2003).

Right to privacy and data protection

Digital mental health tools and school-based monitoring technologies raise ongoing concerns about privacy, surveillance, and data misuse, potentially undermining autonomy and confidentiality rights (UNICEF, 2021; Mittelstadt et al., 2016).

[3] Barriers to Acceptable, Affordable, and Effective Care (with Focus on Marginalized Youth)

Systems-level and legal barriers

Many countries lack stand-alone child and adolescent mental health (CAMH) legislation or enforceable rights-based policies, limiting accountability for equitable access and quality (WHO, 2021). Mental health services remain hospital-centred and urban-focused, excluding rural and low-income youth, while privatized or fee-based models create de facto discrimination (Patel et al., 2018).

Service design and availability

Youth-specific services are frequently under-resourced and adult-centric, failing to meet developmental needs (McGorry et al., 2014). Integrated and early-intervention models show promise but remain unevenly implemented, particularly in LMICs (WHO, 2022).

Barriers affecting marginalized groups

LGBTQI+ youth report lack of affirming services and hostile policy environments as major determinants of poor mental health (The Trevor Project, 2023). Racialized and Indigenous youth experience long wait times, lack of culturally competent care, and mistrust rooted in historical and ongoing discrimination (Dryden & Nnorom, 2021).

Community-level barriers

Stigma, low mental health literacy, and fear of breaches of confidentiality reduce help-seeking, while unsupportive school and family environments limit pathways to care (Corrigan et al., 2014).

[4] Digital Systems and Human Rights Impacts

Mental health impacts

Systematic and umbrella reviews find small and inconsistent associations between overall social media use and adolescent mental health, with both risks and benefits documented (Odgers & Jensen, 2020). Evidence does not support claims that screen time alone causes widespread clinical harm, but highlights elevated risks associated with cyberbullying, problematic use, and exposure to harmful content, particularly among vulnerable youth (Orben, 2020).

Platform design and surveillance

Algorithmic curation can amplify harmful content, including self-harm and hate speech, while academic surveillance technologies often lack adequate privacy safeguards (UNICEF, 2021). These systems may disproportionately affect marginalized youth, reinforcing inequality.

Regulatory responses

Evidence suggests that safety-by-design, transparency requirements, and independent oversight are more effective than blanket age bans, which may be ineffective or counterproductive (Livingstone et al., 2023).

[5] Laws, Policies, and Programs with Evidence of Effectiveness

Rights-based mental health system strengthening aligned with WHO’s Universal Health Coverage for Mental Health framework is associated with more equitable service provision (WHO, 2021). School-based universal interventions show modest benefits for anxiety, while targeted early-intervention approaches demonstrate stronger evidence for high-risk youth (McGorry et al., 2014). Digital mental health interventions can expand access but require stronger evidence on effectiveness, equity, and privacy (WHO, 2022).

[6] Policy Gaps Identified in the Literature

  • Lack of enforceable CAMH legislation and accountability mechanisms
  • Underrepresentation of LMICs and marginalized youth in research
  • Weak global governance of youth data protection and algorithmic accountability
  • Insufficient evaluation of widely adopted school-based and digital programs

[7] Evidence-Based Policy Recommendations for OHCHR

  • Recognize youth mental health as a justiciable component of the right to health and non-discrimination.
  • Encourage States to adopt rights-based CAMH legislation defining entitlements to youth-appropriate, affordable care.
  • Promote youth-inclusive mental health impact assessments for major education, economic, and digital policies.
  • Support investment in community- and school-based youth mental health systems, with protected CAMH funding.
  • Strengthen human-rights-based governance of digital and AI systems, including safety-by-design, transparency, and independent audits.
  • Address structural discrimination through inclusive, culturally competent, and affirming services for marginalized youth.
  • Institutionalize safe, meaningful youth participation with mental health safeguards and monitoring.
